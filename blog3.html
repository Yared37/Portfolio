Embarrassment as an Unexplored Emotion

January 5, 2025 • 8 min read
Psychology Emotions Social

Let's talk about embarrassment.

We know grief, we know anger, we know joy. They get essays, books, podcasts. Embarrassment? It hides. It sits in the corner and laughs quietly when you stumble over your words, forget someone's name, or wave at someone who wasn't waving at you.

Think about the last time you felt it. Close your eyes. That little heat in your chest. That sudden wish to disappear. Now ask yourself: how often does embarrassment guide your choices without you realizing it?

It's everywhere. The way we pause before sending a text, how we hesitate to ask a question, the way we avoid eye contact in a room full of people who probably won't even notice us.

Embarrassment is a whisper, but it shapes us more than we know. And maybe that's the magic of it. A quiet force that makes life more tender, more human, more alive.

Brené Brown has built an empire talking about shame and vulnerability. She's written five books on the subject. But even she tends to gloss over embarrassment's smaller, weirder cousin. Shame is heavy, existential, about your core worth. Embarrassment is lighter but somehow more insidious. It's about being seen as foolish in a specific moment. And it's constantly running in the background of your consciousness.

Darwin wrote about embarrassment in The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals. He noticed that blushing is uniquely human. No other animal does it. He called it "the most peculiar and most human of all expressions." We physically announce our embarrassment to others. Our bodies betray us. Why would evolution design us this way?

One theory is that blushing is a nonverbal apology. A way of saying "I know I messed up, please don't reject me from the group." It's a social repair mechanism. But it also means we're hardwired to broadcast our most uncomfortable moments.

There's this scene in Fleabag where Phoebe Waller-Bridge's character does something mortifying and then breaks the fourth wall to tell the audience, "I'll think about that moment at least once a week for the rest of my life." Everyone who's seen it nods in recognition. We all have those moments. That cringe archive stored in perfect detail.

Psychologists call them "cringe attacks." Those sudden intrusive memories of past embarrassments that make you physically recoil. You're driving to work and suddenly you remember that thing you said wrong at a party in 2015 and you actually say "oh god" out loud to no one.

Why does the brain do this? What purpose could it possibly serve to replay humiliating moments years after they happened? Some researchers think it's a form of social rehearsal. Your brain is reviewing what went wrong so you don't do it again. But that explanation feels insufficient. The moments never really help you. They just hurt.

In the show The Office, basically every episode runs on secondhand embarrassment. Michael Scott does something so cringe-inducing that viewers have to pause the show. There are entire threads online about people who can't watch certain episodes because the embarrassment is too visceral. We feel it in our bodies even when it's happening to a fictional character.

That tells you something about how powerful embarrassment is. It's contagious. Empathetic. You can catch it from watching someone else experience it.

It also reminds me that learning, connection, and growth often happen in discomfort. In classrooms, in friendships, in moments where we're unsure or exposed, embarrassment pushes us to notice, to reflect, to improve. It's not fun, but it's vital.

Sociologist Erving Goffman spent his career studying embarrassment. His book The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life is all about how we manage our public image to avoid humiliation. He argued that all social interaction is essentially a performance where we're trying to maintain "face." Embarrassment is what happens when our performance fails and our real, flawed self shows through.

But here's what's interesting. Goffman noticed that people who never experience embarrassment tend to be either very young children or sociopaths. Embarrassment requires a theory of mind. You need to understand that other people are perceiving you and judging you. It's actually a sophisticated social emotion.

Which makes it weird that we treat it as trivial. Like it's the shallow end of the emotional pool. When actually it might be one of the most complex feelings we have.

The Japanese have a concept called "haji" which encompasses embarrassment, shame, and social disgrace. It's so central to the culture that avoiding haji shapes major life decisions. There are entire social protocols designed to help people save face and prevent embarrassment. Western cultures tend to be more individualistic, but the force is still there. We just don't talk about it as much.

Stand-up comedy is basically monetized embarrassment. Comedians tell stories of their most humiliating moments and we laugh in recognition and relief that it didn't happen to us. The best comedy makes you feel like the comedian is revealing something true and universal about human awkwardness.

In his special Disgraceful, Tom Segura has this bit about running into someone he knows but can't place. The whole neurotic internal monologue about whether to admit you don't remember them or fake it. Everyone watching goes "oh god, yes." Because we've all been there. We've all felt that specific flavor of social panic.

David Sedaris built his entire writing career on embarrassment. His essays are catalogs of mortifying moments rendered in perfect, painful detail. But by turning embarrassment into art, he transforms it. The confession becomes connection. The humiliation becomes humor.

Maybe that's one way to process it. Not to eliminate embarrassment but to acknowledge it. To stop pretending we're the only ones carrying around a highlight reel of our worst moments. To share them and realize everyone else has their own collection.

Because right now, embarrassment operates in silence. It shapes everything but we never name it. We organize our lives around avoiding it without admitting that's what we're doing.

What would you do differently if you weren't afraid of looking stupid? If embarrassment didn't have veto power over your choices? If you could just be awkward and move on?

Probably a lot.

Maybe embarrassment deserves the same respect we give other emotions. Not as something to eliminate but as something to understand. To sit with. To stop letting it control the show from the shadows.

Because right now, it's running the whole operation.

And you didn't even realize it was in charge.
